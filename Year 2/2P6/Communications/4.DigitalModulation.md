A transmitter encodes (adds redundancy to the source bits to protect against noise) and modulates (transforms the coded bits into waveforms)
A receiver demodulates back to bits, then decodes to recover the source bits

We will study a digital baseband modulation technique called Pulse Amplitude Modulation (PAM)

Firstly, we map from bits to real/complex numbers called symbols. The set of symbols the bits are mapped to is called the constellation
In a constellation with M symbols, each symbol represents $\log_2M$ bits

**The Pulse Shape**
We then multiple the symbols by shifted unit-energy baseband waveforms $p(t)$, such as a sinc pulse or a rect pulse. 
If we have constellation symbols $X_0,X_1,...$, then $x_b(t)=\sum_k X_kp(t-kT)$
T is the symbol time of the pulse

The transmission rate is 1/T symbols/sec or $\log_2 M / T$ bits/sec

**Desirable Properties of p(t)**
1) We want it to decay quickly in time
2) We want it to be band limited
$X_b(f)=P(f)\sum_k X_k e^{j2\pi fkT}$ so the bandwidth of $x_b(t)$ is the same as that of $p(t)$
3) The retrieval of the information should be simple and reliable

We really desire the orthonormal shifts property. That is:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}p(t-KT)p(t-mT)dt$ is 1 if $k=m$ and $0$ if $k \neq m$
This is similar to saying that the pulses don't overlap, but not quite, as the sinc function also has this property

A sinc function decays slow in time ($\approx 1/t$) but perfectly band limited
A rect is perfectly limited in time but decays slowly in frequency $\approx 1/f$

In practice, we use a root raised cosine which combines the benefits of both
Bandidth slightly larger than $1/(2T)$ and decay in time of $\approx 1/t^2$

**PAM Demodulation**
We need to use a matched filter with impulse response $h(t)=p(-t)$
This gives a waveform of $x_b(t)*h(t)=\sum_k X_k \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}p(\tau-kT)p(\tau-t)d\tau$

This is $\sum_k X_k g(t-kT)$ where $g(t)= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}p(u+t)p(u)du$, so has the same form as the PAM signal, except the pulse is now $g(t)$

Then, when we sample at $mT$, due to the orthonormal shift property of $p(t)$ we get $X_m$ popping out, our symbols 

**Noise**

We assume noise is Gaussian with a mean of zero and a variance of $\sigma^2$
Noise can make it's way into the system in the form of $Y_m=X_m+N_m$
Where $N_m=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}n(\tau)p(\tau-mT)d\tau$

Given Y, how to we know X? We use a maximum-likelihood encoder, which maximised the probability of $P(Y|X=A)$. 
This corresponds to simply choosing the constellation symbol which is closest to the output Y

**Probability of an Error**
$P_e=P(\hat{X}\neq X)=\frac{1}{2}P(N>A)+\frac{1}{2}P(N<-A)$
Due to the symmetry of the Gaussian, this is simply $P(N>A)=P(\frac{N}{\sigma}>\frac{A}{\sigma})$, standardised
This can be written as $Q(\frac{A}{\sigma})$, noting that $Q(x)=1-\Phi(x)$

$P_e=Q(\frac{A}{\sigma})=Q(\sqrt{\frac{E_S}{\sigma^2}})$
Where $E_S$ is the average energy per symbol of the constellation
For an $M$-point constellation, the average energy per symbol $E_S=E_b \log_2 M$

The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is given by $E_S/\sigma^2$
$Q(x)\approx e^{-x^2/2}$ for large x, so $P_e\approx e^{-SNR/2}$
1 symbol transmitted every T seconds with average energy $E_S$ so transmit power is $E_S/T=E_b\log_2 M / T$
If you want to decrease error, you have to increase SNR, but that drains your battery faster

**Passband PAM**

The natural thing to do to make this passband would be to modulate some high frequency carrier with $x_b(t)$
The up converted PAM$x(t)=x_b(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) = [\sum_k X_kp(t-kT)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$
$X(f)=\frac{1}{2}[X_b(f-f_c)+X_b(f+f_c)]$

Up converted PAM with pulse rect($\frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$) is called Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK)
Not bandwidth efficient

As from before, we know that we can recover $x_b(t)$ by multiplying again by the carrier then using a low pass filter $[-W,W]$. This results in $y_b(t)=x_b(t)+n_b(t)$ where $n_b(t)$ is the baseband noise.
Next we demodulate this signal, which is the same as before, pass through a matched filter then sample at $mT$

**But we can do better!**
Up converted PAM is very wasteful of bandwidth

Remember that $x_b(t)$ is a real signal as the pulse and the symbols are real $X_b(-f)=X_b^*(f)$
Remember also that sending both sidebands is redundant as all the information is stored in one
If I know the spectrum for positive frequencies I know that for negative frequencies

We could just send one sideband, but as we're sending digital information we can do something more clever. Make the information symbols complex valued.
The symbols in the constellation, $X_m$, are complex

$x_b(t)$ is now complex, but the passband signal we transmit must be real, so we generate it as:
$x(t)=Re[x_b(t)e^{j2\pi f_c t}]=Re[x_b(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)-Im[x_b(t)]\sin(2\pi f_c t)$
This is called Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM)

There is now fresh information in both sidebands
The upconverted QAM signal is $x(t)=\sum_k p(t-kT)[Re[x_b(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)-Im[x_b(t)]\sin(2\pi f_c t)]$
$= \sum_k p(t-kT)|X_k|\cos(2\pi f_c t + \phi_k)$
So you can either think of it as QAM having two carriers, the cosine carries real and sin carries imaginary part, or you can think that the symbol modulates the amplitude and phase of the carrier, whereas in upconverted PAM it's just the amplitude

Phase shift keying (PSK) - the magnitude of $X_k$ is fixed and the information is in the phase of it. For all PSK constellations, the average symbol energy $E_s=A^2$
In a constellation with M symbols, each symbol is $\log_2 M$ bits
Average energy per bit $E_b=E_s/\log_2 M$

For QAM, we need two product modulators, which modulate separate copies of the incoming waveform, one with cos and one with sine, then both go through a low pass filter. This gives us:

$y^r(t)=\sum_k X_k^r p(t-kT)+n^r(t)$
$y^i(t)=\sum_k X_k^i p(t-kT)+n^i(t)$
We then put them both through a matched filter then sampler, giving us real part and imaginary part separately, plus noise of course

The optimal detector is one which chooses the symbol closest to the observed output
There is a trade off between rate, power, and error probability

$P_e$ decays exponentially with $d^2/\sigma^2$ where d is the separation between adjacent constellation points
If we increase the number of constellation points, we increase transmission rate but increase power too

We could also modulate the frequency of a digital signal with frequency shift keying (FSK) but we won't consider that here

**Channel Coding**

Assume the modulator, channel and demodulator section are just a binary channel with a probability p of flipping every bit that passes through it.
How to add redundancy to protect against error?

Repetition code - each bit repeated a certain number of times
Rate is 1/n where n is the number of repetitions
$P_e$ goes to zero as n increases, but the rate also goes to zero
Claude Shannon showed it was possible to have a fixed rate while sending $P_e$ to zero
The capacity is the maximum rate at which the error probability can be made arbitrarily small

Block codes -
Hamming code (4,7)
If the source block is ($s_1, s_2, s_3, s_4$)
We add three bits, $c_5 = s_1 \oplus s_2 \oplus s_3$, $c_6 = s_2 \oplus s_3 \oplus s_4$, $c_7 = s_1 \oplus s_3 \oplus s_4$

0 is even parity, 1 is odd parity
We essentially have a three ring venn diagram, where we are ensuring that each ring is even parity. When a code arrives, we can flip one bit such that this is true.
This only works when either none or just one bit was flipped in the binary channel

The rate is 4/7

**Multiple Access**
There are three ways to dela with the problem of multiple access:
1) Each signal comes one after the other, with the whole bandwidth. Time division multiple access - TDMA
2) FDMA - All at the same time, but with different frequencies, so using a different fraction of available bandwidth
3) CDMA - All at the same time with whole bandwidth, but each has a distinct signature

In FDMA, when they all arrive, multiply each by the respective carrier $\cos(2\pi f_{ci} t)$ then low pass filter it in band $[-B/K, B/K]$ where B is the available bandwidth and K is the number of users

In CDMA, each user is given a mutually orthogonal signature, so $\int_{mT}^{(m+1)T}C_i(t)C_j(t)dt$ is 1 for i=j and 0 otherwise
The signals of K users are multiplexed as such: $s^{CDMA}(t)=\sum_{i=1}^K c_i(t)x_i(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t)$

After product modulator and low pass filter: $y(t)=\sum_{i=1}^K c_i(t)x_i(t)+ \text{noise}$
To recover $X_m^j$: multiply by $c_j(t)$ and integrate over the $m$th period $[mT, (m+1)T]$
