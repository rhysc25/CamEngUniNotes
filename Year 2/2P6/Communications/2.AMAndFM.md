If we work out the spectrum of an AM signal $[a_0+x(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$, we get:
$$S_{AM}(f)=\frac{a_0}{2}[\delta(f-f_c)+\delta (f+f_c)]+\frac{1}{2}[X(f-f_c)+X (f+f_c)]$$
This has a passband spectrum and a delta pulse
The bandwidth is $2W$ and the power is $P_{AM}=\frac{a_0^2}{2}+\frac{P_X}{2}$ where $P_X$ is the power of $x(t)$

**DSB-SC**

To reduce the power needed, we can remove $a_0^2/2$ by suppressing the carrier. Therefore we have double sideband suppressed carrier (DSB-SC)
The DSB-SC waveform is $s_{dsb-sc}(t)=x(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t)$
We can't use envelope detection to recover the signal, so we have to multiply by a copy of the carrier signal at the receiver, giving:$v(t)=\frac{x(t)}{2}+x(t)\frac{\cos(4\pi f_c t)}{2}$
This puts a copy of $X(f)$ centred around $f=0$.

Properties of DSB-SC:
The bandwidth is $2W$, and the power is $P_X/2$. It requires less power as the carrier is not transmitted. However, the receiver is more complicated. The carrier signal on the receiver side must be synchronised perfected with the transmitter's carrier.

**SSB-SC**

To further reduce power, and to reduce bandwidth we only need to transmit half of the passband signal, as $X(-f)=X^*(f)$ means it is symmetrical about $f_c$
The bandwidth is now W, and the power is now $P_X/4$
However the transmitter is much more complicated. The receiver is the same however, requiring a product modulator and a low-pass filter.

**FM**

Encodes the signal by changing the frequency of the carrier. 
The instantaneous frequency $f(t)$ is varied linearly with $x(t)$: $f(t)=f_c+k_f x(t)$

This translated to an instantaneous phase: $$\theta(t)=2\pi\int_0^t f(u)du=2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f \int_0^t x(u) du$$
The modulated FM signal:
$s_{FM}(t)=A_c\cos(\theta(t))=A_c\cos(2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f \int_0^t x(u) du)$
$A_c$ is the carrier amplitude
$k_f$ is called the frequency-sensitivity factor

Demodulation - Take the derivative, then envelope detection:
$\frac{ds_{FM}(t)}{dt}=-2\pi A_c[f_c+k_f x(t)]\sin(2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f \int_0^t x(u) du)$

The power of the FM signal is $A_c^2/2$ regardless of $x(t)$
Non linear: $FM(x_1(t)+x_2(t))\neq FM(x_1(t))+FM(x_2(t))$
It is more robust against additive noise, at the cost of increases transmission bandwidth

**FM Modulation of a Tone**
Consider FM modulation of a tone $x(t)=a_x\cos(2\pi f_x t)$:
$f(t)=f_c+k_f a_x \cos(2\pi f_x t)$
$\theta(t)=2\pi f_c t+ \frac{k_f a_x}{f_x}\sin(2\pi f_x t)$
$\Delta f=f_f a_x$ is called the frequency deviation, the max deviation of the carrier frequency $f(t)$ from $f_c$

$\beta = \frac{\Delta f}{f_x}$ is called the modulation index, the max deviation of the carrier phase from $2\pi f_c t$
$s_{FM}(t)=A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t+\beta \sin(2\pi f_x t))$
Fourier transform $\rightarrow$ $S_{FM}(f)=\frac{A_c}{2}\sum_{-\infty}^{\infty}J_n(\beta) [\delta(f-f_c-nf_x)+\delta (f+f_c+nf_x)]$
Where $J_n(\beta)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}e^{j(\beta \sin u -nu)}$, the nth order Bessel function of the first kind.
This decays to zero so the deltas become less and less significant

There is a carrier component at $f_c$, and components located symmetrically on either side of $f_c$ at $f_c\pm f_x, f_c\pm2f_x,...$
The absolute bandwidth is infinite but the side components become negligible further and further from $f_c$
Carson's rule for the effective bandwidth:
The bandwidth of an FM signal generated by modulating a single tone is $B_{FM}\approx 2\Delta f + 2f_x=2\Delta f(1+\frac{1}{\beta})$
For an FM signal generated by modulating a general signal $x(t)$ with bandwidth $W$, the bandwidth, $B_{FM}\approx 2\Delta f+2W$
