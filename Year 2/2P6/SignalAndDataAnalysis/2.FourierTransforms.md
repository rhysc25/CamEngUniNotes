Fourier transforms generalise to non-periodic signals
The general principle is to take a slice of the signal and repeat it, as though it was periodic. This will give a frequency spectrum. We then vary T, increasing to infinity, until the frequency spectrum is more like a smooth function (or delta functions).

As $T\rightarrow \infty$, we obtain a component at every frequency $\omega$

We define a function $D_T(\omega)$ which equals $Tc_n$ at $\omega = n\omega_0$
$D_T(\omega)=\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}f(t)e^{-j\omega t}dt$
We do not average over T so that we have a meaningful limit
The Fourier transform is defined as this as $T\rightarrow \infty$
$$F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-j\omega t}dt$$
This produces components for all frequencies
The Fourier transform decomposes a signal into an integral of sinusoids, and $F(\omega)$ provides the weights of these components

**The Inverse Fourier Transform**
From $F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t')e^{-j\omega t'}dt'$
Multiply both sides by $e^{j\omega t}$ and integrate over $\omega$
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega)d\omega=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t')e^{-j\omega t'}e^{j\omega t}dt'd\omega$
Switch the summation around, giving:
$I=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t')\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{j\omega (t - t')}d\omega dt'$
This gives that:
$I=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t') 2\pi \delta(t-t') dt'=2\pi f(t)$
Therefore, the inverse Fourier transform is:
$$f(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega)e^{j\omega t}d\omega$$
Important Fourier Transforms:

* If $f(t)=e^{j\omega_0 t} \Rightarrow \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-j(\omega - \omega_0)t}dt = 2\pi \delta(\omega - \omega_0)$
* For sinusoids, convert to complex exponential form then do superposition of Fourier transforms, as they are linear.
* If $f(t)=a\delta(t)$, by the sifting property, $F(\omega)=a$. This is a constant for all frequencies.
* If $f(t)=a$, then $F(\omega)=2\pi a f(\omega)$
* For a rectangular pulse $f(t) = b$ for $|t| < T/2$, $F(\omega)=bT sinc(\frac{\omega T}{2})$
* If $f(t)=e^{-a^2 t^2}$, then $F(\omega)=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{a}e^{\frac{-\omega^2}{4a^2}}$. Increasing a makes $f(t)$ narrower and $F(\omega)$ broader

**Fourier Transform Intuition**
$e^{-j\omega t}$ is a test oscillation at frequency $\omega$.
The FT forms a weighted sum of $f(t)$ against this oscillation.
If the oscillation matches part of $f(t)$ the contributions reinforce so $|F(\omega)|$ is large

**Properties of the Fourier Transform**

**Linearity**
$f(t)=af_1(t)+bf_2(t) \longleftrightarrow aF_1(\omega) + bF_2(\omega) = F(\omega)$
This enables superposition

**Time Scaling / Similarity Theorem**
$$f(\alpha t) \longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{\alpha}F(\frac{\omega}{\alpha})$$
If we contract in the time domain we must stretch in the frequency domain.
The uncertainty principle - a function narrow in time is broad in frequency

Heisenberg-Gabor Principle:
If a signal $f(t)$ has time duration T, and its Fourier transform $F(\omega)$ has frequency bandwidth B, then $TB \geq 1$

**Time Shift**
$$f(t-t_0)\longleftrightarrow F(\omega) e^{-j \omega t_0}$$
**Frequency Shift**
$$e^{j\omega_0 t}f(t)\longleftrightarrow F(\omega -\omega_0)$$
Modulation: Information is shifted to higher frequencies using a carrier wave. The spectrum is copied, halved in amplitude and shifted to $\pm \omega_0$

**Differentiation wrt t**
$$\frac{df}{dt}\longleftrightarrow j\omega F(\omega)$$$$f^{(n)}(t) \longleftrightarrow (j\omega)^n F(\omega)$$**Duality**
If $g(\omega)$ is the FT of $f(t)$, then:
$$g(t) \longleftrightarrow 2\pi f(-\omega)$$
This can be proven by substituting t by $-\omega '$ and renaming the integration variable $\omega \rightarrow t'$ in this equation: $f(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}g(\omega)e^{j\omega t}d\omega$
Exchanging the roles of time and frequency produces the original function, reflected and scaled.

**Multiplication Theory**
Consider the integral of the product of $f_1(t)$ and $f^*_2(t)$.
With some substitution and interchanging of the order of integration, we get:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_1(t)f^*_2(t) dt = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F_1(\omega) F^*_2 (\omega) d\omega$

If we set $f_1(t)=f_2(t)$ we get Parseval's Theorem: $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(t)|^2 dt = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |F(\omega)|^2 d\omega$$
The total energy of a signal can be computed either in the time domain or in the frequency domain

If we are considering the amount of energy which lies between frequencies $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$, we must multiply it by two to consider also negative frequencies, so $2 \times \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{\omega_1}^{\omega_2} |F(\omega)|^2 d\omega$
It can also be written using ordinary frequency f, using $d\omega = 2\pi df$

This is useful as:
The Fourier transform preserves total signal energy
The total energy may be more easily computed using the Fourier transform instead of the real signal

**Convolution**
If $h(t)=f * g$, then $H(\omega)=F(\omega)G(\omega)$
This is almost identical to the Laplace transform version, just that is spans positive to negative infinity
By duality: $2\pi f(t) g(t) \longleftrightarrow F(\omega) * G(\omega)$

Convolution in time $\longleftrightarrow$ multiplication in frequency
Multiplication in time $\longleftrightarrow$ convolution in frequency
This is important as convolution via FFT reduces time complexity down from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N\log(N))$

If we have input x and output y, we find the output by convolving the input with the impulse response. This gives $Y(\omega)=X(\omega)H(\omega)$
$H(\omega)$ here is the ratio of the output to the input spectrum

**Relationship with Laplace Transforms** 
For a causal signal $f(t) \quad (f(t)=0 \quad \text{for} \quad t < 0)$, then: $F(\omega) = \bar{f}(j\omega)$
Laplace transforms are better suited to problems with initial conditions at $t=0$
Fourier transforms are better suited to steady-state and frequency-domain analysis
