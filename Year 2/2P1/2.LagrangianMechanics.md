Number of DoFs: number of coordinates needed to describe the configuration of a system
Generalised coordinates are not unique: any N degrees of freedom system would require a set of minimum N generalised coordinates to fully describe its motion

The Lagrangian method is derived from the fact that $F=\frac{d}{dt}(p)$

Consider a system with N DoFs described by a convenient set of generalised coordinates: 
$q_1,q_2,q_3,...,q_N$

The Lagrangian is the difference between the kinetic (T) and potential (V) energies.
$L(q_j,\dot{q}_j)=T-V$
The Lagrangian is parameterised in terms of $q$ and $\dot{q}$, which are treated as two independent parameters
$$\frac{d}{dt}(p_j)=F_j$$
Where $p_j=\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_j}$ and $F_j=\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j}$

To write the Lagrange equations, we assumed the system is holonomic. I.e. the current state of the system can be fully described by the instantaneous displacements and velocities, i.e. the displacements and velocities are independent at a given instant in time.

The Lagrangian for two independent systems is the sum of the independent sub-Lagrangians.
It produces multiple independent equations of motion.

Convenient coordinates for masses connected by a spring is the distance between masses $x_s=x_2-x_1$, and the coordinate of the centre of mass $x_G=\frac{m_1x_1+m_2x_2}{m_1+m_2}$
Solving using these shows that the total momentum of the two masses is conserved as $\frac{d}{dt}(p_{x_g})=0$

Noether's Theorem:
If the Lagrangian doesn't depend on generalised coordinate $q_i$, then the generalised momentum $p_i$ is conserved

Linear momentum is conserved when the Lagrangian system is invariant under a translational offset in space, i.e. you can move it left and right and it acts the same
Angular momentum is conserved when the Lagrangian system is invariant under an offset in angle

When the Lagrangian doesn't explicitly depend on time, we know that energy is conserved.
$\frac{d}{dt}L(q_j, \dot{q}_j)=0 \Rightarrow E = T+V$. Energy conservation is the first integral of the equation of motion.
$\frac{1}{2}m\dot{q}^2+V(q)=E \Rightarrow \dot{q}=\sqrt{\frac{2}{m}(E-V(q))}$

The motion is unbounded and non periodic if E is greater than two separate peaks of $V(q)$

Equilibrium states:
No motion when $\dot{q}=\ddot{q}=0, q=q_0$
We know that this occurs at a stationary point of the potential energy: $V'(q_0)=0$
We also know that this is when $E=V(q_0)$
For a stable point of equilibrium $V''(q_0)>0$

Phase portraits: A plot describing the motion with $\dot{q}$ against $q$
E.g. roughly circular for a small angle pendulum
If $E>V_{max}$ then the lines are infinite as they never hit $\dot{q}=0$
On a phase portrait, a stable equilibrium point is shown as a dot in a circle, whereas an unstable equilibrium point is shown as a cross

Considering the first integral: $\dot{q}=\sqrt{\frac{2}{m}(E-V(q))}$, if we integrate this a second time, we get an expression for the time to move from state $q_i$ to $q_f$
$t_{q_i\rightarrow q_f}=\int_{q_i}^{q_f}\frac{\sqrt{m/2}}{\sqrt{E-V(q)}}dq$

**Effective Potential**
Steps for finding effective potential:
1) Identify a convenient set of generalised coordinates
2) Calculate T and V
3) Find the Lagrangian
4) Derive the EoM using the Lagrangian
5) Exploit conservation laws for one of the variables. Eliminate said variable by substitution, leaving one variable
6) Rewrite the EoM in standard form equivalent to a one d.o.f motion in an effective potential. $$m\ddot{q}=-V_{eff}'(q)$$
7) Need to obtain the effective potential by integrating (no constant as potential is arbitrary) $$V_{eff}(q)=\int V'_{eff}(q)dq$$
8) Analyse the equivalent single degree of freedom system to understand motion in q.

Once we are at this point $m\ddot{q}=-V_{eff}'(q)$, we can apply small perturbation analysis to get the equation of motion:
$r=r_{min}+\delta r$. $m(\ddot{r}_{min}+\ddot{\delta r})=m\ddot{\delta r}=V'_{eff}(r_{min}+\delta r)$
Taylor expand this term $V'_{eff}(r_{min}+\delta r)$

If we have a two body problem, we can use their position vectors $\textbf{r}_i$. Then we can have our two generalised coordinates as the centre of mass and the separation. 
$\textbf{r}_G=\frac{m_1\textbf{r}_1+m_2\textbf{r}_2}{m_1+m_2}$, and $\textbf{r}_s=\textbf{r}_2-\textbf{r}_1$
The first one demonstrates the conservation of total momentum, therefore we are able to write this in terms of one coordinate.

**Small Amplitude Vibration for MDOFs**
Strategy 1: Linearise the equations of motion
1) Define generalised coordinates
2) Use Lagrangian mechanics to find the EoMs 
3) Find equilibrium points, where $q=q_0, \dot{q}=\ddot{q}=0$
4) Expand/linearise the equations of motion
5) Identify an equation of the form $M\delta \ddot{q}+K\delta q=0$
6) Assume simple harmonic motion $\delta q=Ae^{i\omega t}$
7) Obtain eigenvalues (natural frequencies) and eigenvectors (mode shapes) $det(-\omega^2M+K)=0$
8) Express the general solution as a sum of these normal modes $\delta q=\sum_{i=1}^n C_i A_i \cos(\omega_i t + \phi_i)$
Don't keep the quadratic terms. $\cos(\phi)\approx 1$

Strategy 2: Linearise the Lagrangian instead
1) $L(q,\dot{q})=T-V\approx \frac{1}{2}\dot{q}^TM\dot{q}-\frac{1}{2}q^TKq=\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\sum_j\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial \dot{q}_i \partial \dot{q}_j}|_0 \dot{q}_i\dot{q}_j-\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\sum_j\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial q_i \partial q}_j|_0 q_iq_j$
Here we maintain the quadratic terms as they can create linear terms later
When we linearise the Lagrangian, we can ignore any constant terms as the potential has an arbitrary datum

The term on the trailing diagonal of the K matrix is multiplied by two in the $Cq_1q_2$ term
