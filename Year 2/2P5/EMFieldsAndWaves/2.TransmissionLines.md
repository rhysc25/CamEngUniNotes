All transmission lines consist of at least two conductors (out path and return path) separated by an insulator
There will be some capacitance between them. The wire also has some inductance. This leads to an equivalent circuit of the form:
![[Pasted image 20260225162011.png]]
This is for a small length of transmission line $dx$, where $L$ and $C$ are per unit length.
Voltage and current along transmission line are a function of position.

Voltage drop across inductor = $V(x+\delta x, t) - V(x,t)=\delta V = -L\delta x \frac{\partial I}{\partial t}$
This rearranges to $\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}=-L\frac{\partial I}{\partial t}$
Similarly for the current: $\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}=-C\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$
These are known as the Telegrapher's Equations

Combining these we have: 
$\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2}=LC\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial t^2}$ and $\frac{\partial^2 I}{\partial x^2}=LC\frac{\partial^2 I}{\partial t^2}$
Which both have the generic form of the wave equation
By comparison we can see that $c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$ where L and C are per unit length

For a coax cable: $C = \frac{2\pi \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r}{\ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)}$ and $L = \frac{\mu_0 \mu_r}{2\pi} \ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ so $c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r \mu_0\mu_r}}$
The speed of the wave along the transmission line is dependent only on the property of the dielectric medium between the conductors. This is always the case for transmission lines.

A solution to the system could be that $V$ is the sum of a forward and backward wave.
$V = V_F \cos(\omega t - \beta x + \Phi_F) + V_B \cos(\omega t + \beta x + \Phi_B)$
$\Phi$ is the phase offset at $t=x=0$

$V=\bar{V}_Fe^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}+ \bar{V}_Be^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}$ and similarly for current $I=\bar{I}_Fe^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}+ \bar{I}_Be^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}$
We now have expressions for voltage and current waves that can pass in either direction along a transmission line. The speed of the wave is determined by the speed of electromagnetic waves in the dielectric

As the direction alternates in a coax cable the electric field points in then out then in, and the circulating magnetic field goes clockwise then anticlockwise then clockwise. These are perpendicular, and also both perpendicular to the direction of the wave propagation.
As the wave propagates along the conductor, it induces charge on the conductors, sustaining the E and H fields.

The electronics in the conductors are only oscillating locally, moving at the drift velocity. (~ cm/s)
The free charge in the conductors is guiding the surrounding electromagnetic wave.

**Real Lossy Transmission Line**
The conductors have a resistance per unit length $R$ and the dielectrics, as they are not perfect, have a conductance per unit length $G$, leading to a new equivalent circuit.
![[Pasted image 20260226113919.png]]
We now have that $V=\bar{V}_Fe^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}e^{-\alpha x}+ \bar{V}_Be^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}e^{\alpha x}$. The wave decays with distance. The second term is going in -x so also decays.
Where $\alpha$ is the attenuation constant.
The propagation constant is therefore: $\alpha + j\beta=\sqrt{(R+j\omega L)(G+j\omega C)}$

Characteristic impedance - 
Considering again the Telegrapher equation $\frac{\partial I}{\partial x}=-C\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$ 
$\left(-\beta \bar{I}_F e^{-j\beta x} + \beta \bar{I}_B e^{j\beta x}\right) = -C\left(\omega \bar{V}_F e^{-j\beta x} + \omega \bar{V}_B e^{j\beta x}\right)$
Separately equating the forward and backward terms give us:
$\frac{\bar{V}_F}{\bar{I}_F}=\frac{\beta}{\omega C}$ and $\frac{\bar{V}_B}{-\bar{I}_B}=\frac{\beta}{\omega C}$
This makes sense as when the wave is travelling in the negative x direction, the current will be negative but the voltage won't.

Therefore the characteristic impedance, the ratio of voltage to current of a unidirectional wave at any point on the transmission line, is $Z_0=\beta / \omega C=\sqrt{L/C}$
It is always real for an ideal lossless line. It does not dissipate power as the I and V aren't between the same points.
It is the apparent impedance that is ‘seen’ if looking into an infinitely long line at 𝑥 = 0. Important for impedance matching.

For a real transmission line: $Z_0=$ Geometry factor $\times \sqrt{\frac{\mu_0\mu_r}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}}$
For a lossy line: $Z_0=\sqrt{\frac{R+j\omega L}{G+j\omega C}}$

For maximum output power in a load we would want $Z_L=Z_0$ to maximise the signal in the coaxial cable.
