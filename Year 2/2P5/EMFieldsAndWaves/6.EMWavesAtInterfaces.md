The intrinsic impedance relates E and H. $\eta = \frac{|E|}{|H|}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0\mu_r}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}}$

Boundary conditions:
The normal components of D are conserved and the tangential components of E are conserved
$D_{n1}=D_{n2}$, $E_{t1}=E_{t2}$
The normal components of B are conserved and the tangential components of H are conserved.
$B_{n1}=B_{n2}$, $H_{t1}=H_{t2}$

A non polarised wave will have many directions of E
We can resolve any arbitrary electromagnetic wave into two components with their electric fields perpendicular to each other

Total internal reflection: $\theta_{ic}=\sin^{-1}(n_2/n_1)$

If the electric field is perpendicular to the plane of incidence:
$\frac{E_t}{E_i}=\frac{2\eta _2 \cos(\theta_i)}{\eta_2 \cos(\theta_i)+\eta_1 \cos(\theta_t)}$
$\frac{E_r}{E_i}=\frac{\eta _2 \cos(\theta_i) - \eta_1 \cos(\theta_t)}{\eta_2 \cos(\theta_i)+\eta_1 \cos(\theta_t)}$

If instead the electric field is parallel to the plane of incidence:
$\frac{E_t}{E_i}=\frac{2\eta _2 \cos(\theta_i)}{\eta_2 \cos(\theta_i)+\eta_1 \cos(\theta_t)}$
$\frac{E_r}{E_i}=\frac{\eta _1 \cos(\theta_i) - \eta_2 \cos(\theta_t)}{\eta_2 \cos(\theta_i)+\eta_1 \cos(\theta_t)}$

The component with the E field perpendicular always gets reflected more. There is an angle, the Brewster angle $\theta_B$, where the component with the E field parallel is not reflected at all
$\theta_B=\tan^{-1}(n_2/n_1)$

Impedance matching - we may need to put an anti-reflection coating on the boundary, such that $\eta_0 = \sqrt{\eta_1\eta_2}$ and thickness $\lambda / 4$
