In conducting media:
$\nabla \cdot D = 0$ as $\rho = 0$, as can be no net charge build up

Ohm's law: $J = \sigma E$

Combining several of the Maxwell equations we get an equation with just E in it
$\nabla^2 E = \mu \sigma \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}+\mu \epsilon \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2}$
This gives a solution: $E = E_{0x}\textbf{i}[exp(j\omega t)exp(-\gamma y)]$
$\gamma$ is the propagation constant that is a complex number $\gamma = \alpha + j\beta$

Subbing our solution in we find that $\gamma = \sqrt{j\mu \omega (\sigma + j\omega \epsilon)}$
The relative magnitude of $\sigma$ to $\omega \epsilon$ tells us whether we need to worry about attenuation effect

If highly resistive: $\alpha=0, \beta = \omega \sqrt{\mu \epsilon}$
If highly conductive: $\alpha = \beta = \sqrt{\frac{\mu \omega \sigma}{2}}$
We define the skin depth as a measure of the distance that an EM wave can penetrate a conducting medium
$\delta = \alpha^{-1}=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu \omega \sigma}}$. Decreases as $\omega$ increases

Only really an outside skin layer of the conducting wire takes part in conduction

We can find H by taking the curl and integrating with respect to time.
We can then find our intrinsic impedance: $\eta = \sqrt{\frac{j\omega \mu}{\sigma + j\omega \epsilon}}$
The intrinsic impedance of a conducting medium is complex so there is a phase difference between the magnetic and electric field waves in a conductor
