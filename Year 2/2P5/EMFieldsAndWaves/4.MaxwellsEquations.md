1) Gauss Law of Electric Fields
Electric field - region of space where a charge experiences a force. $\textbf{F}=q\textbf{E}$
The lines begin and end on negative charges, or circulate $\oint_S \textbf{E} \cdot \textbf{dA}=\frac{Q}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}$
If a flux line passes in and out of the volume then it has no net effect on the integration. It will only be non zero if a flux line starts or ends in the volume, so on a charge.
Therefore Gauss law just states that charge produces electric fields

The change in potential = $=\textbf{F}\cdot \textbf{dx}=q\cdot dV \Rightarrow \textbf{E}=-\nabla V$
The lines of E always meet surfaces of constant potential perpendicularly

Using the Divergence Theorem we can rewrite this as $\int_V (\nabla \cdot \textbf{E})dV=\frac{1}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}\int_V \rho \cdot dV$. Therefore, $\nabla \cdot \textbf{E}=\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}$, which is Gauss' law in differential form. To make this independent of material we can say: $$\nabla\cdot \textbf{D}=\rho$$
2) Gauss Law of Magnetic Fields
There are no magnetic monopoles, only dipoles, so lines of magnetic flux density $\textbf{B}$ form closed loops. Therefore all that goes into a volume must come out, so $$\nabla \cdot \textbf{B}=0$$
3) Faraday Law of Magnetic Fields
We know that $V=-\frac{d\phi}{dt}$. 
Also, if there is a potential difference along the wire then there must be an electric field in the direction of the wire. If we imagine a loop of wire then we can integrate this field around the wire to give the potential difference around the closed loop.
![[Pasted image 20260304123135.png]]
$\oint_C \textbf{E}\cdot d\textbf{r}=V$
By equating the two and writing $\phi=\int_S \textbf{B}\cdot d\textbf{A}$ the equation becomes:
$\oint_C \textbf{E}\cdot d\textbf{r}=-\int_S\frac{d\textbf{B}}{dt}\cdot d\textbf{A}$
Using stokes theorem we can write that: $$\nabla \times \textbf{E}=-\frac{\partial \textbf{B}}{\partial t}$$
A changing magnetic field will induce an electric field. This is a circulating electric field, and no charge is required for its production.

4) The Ampere-Maxwell Law
The Ampere Law states that $\oint_C \textbf{H}\cdot d\textbf{r}=I$
Using Stokes theorem and the fact that $I=\int_S \textbf{J}\cdot d\textbf{A}$, we can say that $\nabla \times \textbf{H}=\textbf{J}$. However, if we consider something like a capacitor, where no current flows, this would state that no magnetic flux exists there. Clearly we need something more, and that something is a displacement current term.

Considering continuity equations for charge, we see that:
$-\int_S \textbf{J}\cdot d\textbf{A}=-\int_V \nabla \cdot \textbf{J}dV=\int_V\frac{\partial \rho(\textbf{r})}{\partial t}dV$ so $\nabla \cdot \textbf{J}=-\frac{\partial \rho (\textbf{r})}{\partial t}=-\nabla \cdot (\frac{\partial \textbf{D}}{\partial t})$ (using Gauss Law of Electric Fields)
Combing the two we get:$$\nabla \times \textbf{H}=\textbf{J}+\frac{\partial \textbf{D}}{\partial t}$$
Both a moving charge and a changing electric flux density induce a magnetic field.

**Electromagnetic Waves**

Electromagnetic waves in dielectrics (e.g. air, glass, plastic) -
There is no free charge in these materials, so $\rho=J=0$
This makes $\nabla \cdot \textbf{D} = 0$ and $\nabla \times \textbf{E}=-\frac{\partial \textbf{B}}{\partial t}$

Taking the curl of the second equation and then using a curl identity gives:
$\nabla(\nabla \cdot \textbf{E})-\nabla^2\textbf{E}=-\frac{\partial }{\partial t}(\nabla \times \textbf{B})$
This just gives $\nabla^2\textbf{E}=\frac{\partial }{\partial t}(\nabla \times \textbf{B})$ as $\nabla \cdot \textbf{E}=0$
A bit more combination and rearrangement gives: $$\nabla^2\textbf{E}=\mu_0\mu_r\epsilon_0\epsilon_r\frac{\partial^2 \textbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$
This is just the wave equation in 3D
This proves that $c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\mu_r\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}}$, so refractive index $n=\sqrt{\mu_r\epsilon_r}=\sqrt{\epsilon_r}$ as most dielectrics have $\mu_r=1$ (both of these vary with frequency, leading to dispersion)
This is identical to the result derived from the transmission line
In the transmission line it is an electromagnetic wave in the space around the conductors, the conductors are simply providing free charge to guide the wave.

A similar result can be derived from taking the curl of the Ampere Maxwell law, giving you $$\nabla^2\textbf{H}=\mu_0\mu_r\epsilon_0\epsilon_r\frac{\partial^2 \textbf{H}}{\partial t^2}$$
To consider this further we will consider a plane wave - propagates in a specific direction, is uniform in the plane perpendicular to the propagation direction.
This can be seen if we are sufficiently far from a point source of electromagnetic waves

A valid plane wave solution is: $\textbf{E}=(E_{0x}\textbf{i}+E_{0y}\textbf{j}+E_{0z}\textbf{k})\text{exp}\{j(\omega t - \beta z)\}$
We know that $\nabla \cdot \textbf{E}=0$ as it is a dielectric, hence: $\frac{\partial E_z}{\partial z}=0$
This gives us that either $E_{0z}$ or $\beta$ is zero. A $\beta$ of zero has an infinite $\lambda$ so just a DC quantity not a wave. Therefore $E_{0z}=0$, so no electric field in direction of propagation.
We also see that $\frac{\partial E_x}{\partial x}=0$ and $\frac{\partial E_y}{\partial y}=0$ so electric field constant perpendicular to the propagation

We now assume that the wave is polarised so that the electric field vector is pointing in the x direction, so $\textbf{E}=E_{0x}\textbf{i}\text{exp}\{j(\omega t - \beta z)\}$. Plane polarised wave.

We want to find what the magnetic field is doing:
We know that $\nabla \times \textbf{E}=-\frac{\partial \textbf{B}}{\partial t}$, so we can just find the curl of $\textbf{E}=E_{0x}\textbf{i}\text{exp}\{j(\omega t - \beta z)\}$, then negate and integrate, giving: $\textbf{H}=H_{0y}\textbf{j}\text{exp}\{j(\omega t - \beta z)\}$
Where: $H_{0y}=\frac{\beta E_{0x}}{\omega \mu_0\mu_r}$, so H and E are related in magnitude

Therefore, the magnetic field strength is perpendicular to both E and the propagation direction.
Also, substituting $\beta/\omega$ gives $H_{0y}=E_{0x}\sqrt{\frac{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}{\mu_0\mu_r}}$

The intrinsic impedance of the medium: $\eta=\frac{|E|}{|H|}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0\mu_r}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}}$
This is the same as the characteristic impedance of the transmission line, except missing the geometric factor
For a vacuum, $\eta \approx 377\Omega$

**The Power in the Wave and the Poynting Vector**
If a field occupies a volume of space then a potential energy is stored
An electromagnetic wave is therefore transmitting energy, and so must have a power density, power per unit area

The Poynting Vector $\textbf{N}=\textbf{E}\times \textbf{H}$
N is clearly in the direction of propagation, and the magnitude is the instantaneous power density. This will be time varying.
As they're perpendicular, it just becomes a normal product, where we can express E and H as real numbers $\textbf{E}=E_{0x}\textbf{i}\cos(\omega t - \beta z)$ and similarly for H

Hence $|\textbf{N}|=E_{0x}H_{0y}\cos^2(\omega t - \beta z)$
This is time varying. It is similar to AC power but with no phase offsets

The average power per unit area: $|\bar{\textbf{N}}|=\frac{|\textbf{E}||\textbf{H}|}{2}=E_{rms}H_{rms}$

**Retarded Potentials**
Before now we have been working with static charges and static currents. But now, say we wanted to know the fields around an antenna, they are going to be time varying and depend on the charge and current at the antenna at previous times.
For this reason we use retarded potentials.

Lets image we have an antenna at $r_0$ and we want to know the potential at some point $r$
![[Pasted image 20260311105058.png]]
We know that $V=-\int Edx$, or $\textbf{E}=-\nabla V$
Taking the grad of this we get Poisson's equation: $\nabla^2V=\frac{-\rho}{\epsilon_0\epsilon_r}$

If we imagine there is some charge distribution not varying with time in a small volume v around the antenna, we can get the potential by integrating this charge to give a Coulombic potential
![[Pasted image 20260311105615.png]]
Now what if it is time varying, we have to take into account the speed of the wave, as it takes some time for the wave to travel from $r_0$ to $r$
Including the time delay we get the retarded electric potential
