Our transmission lines are always terminated at some kind of load, $Z_L$ at $x=0$ (the transmission line is in negative x)
As we have defined it to be at $x=0$, we have that $V(0,t)=(\bar{V}_F+\bar{V}_B)e^{j\omega t}$ and $I(0,t)=(\bar{I}_F+\bar{I}_B)e^{j\omega t}$
Therefore we can say that the load impedance is $Z_L=\frac{(\bar{V}_F+\bar{V}_B)}{(\bar{I}_F+\bar{I}_B)}=Z_0\frac{(\bar{V}_F+\bar{V}_B)}{(\bar{V}_F-\bar{V}_B)}$

Rearranging we can find the voltage reflection coefficient $\rho_L=\frac{\bar{V}_B}{\bar{V}_F}=\frac{Z_L-Z_0}{Z_L+Z_0}$
A proportion $\rho_L$ of the forward wave $V_F$ is being reflected to give a backward wave $V_B$ and these two sum together to produce a standing wave

The voltage standing wave ratio is the max amp / min amp = VSWR = $\frac{(|\bar{V}_F|+|\bar{V}_B|)}{(|\bar{V}_F|-|\bar{V}_B|)}=\frac{1+|\rho_L|}{1-|\rho_L|}$
As power is proportional to $V^2$ then the incident power reflected is $|\rho_L|^2$

Three important cases:
1) Open circuit, $Z_L=\infty$ and $\rho_L=1$. The incident wave is reflected with no change in phase. Perfect standing wave (VSWR $=\infty$) formed, antinode as reflection point
2) Closed circuit, $Z_L=0$ and $\rho_L=-1$. The incident wave is reflected with a $\pi$ phase change. Perfect standing wave (VSWR $= \infty$) formed, node at reflection point
3) Impedance matching, $Z_L=Z_0$. $\rho_L=0$. No wave reflected and power dissipated in the load. This is the ideal case

**Connecting Transmission Lines**
If we connect two transmission lines, we get $\rho_L=\frac{Z_{02}-Z_{01}}{Z_{02}+Z_{01}}$
The transmission coefficient is the proportion of the forward voltage in the first transmission line that is transmitted into the second
$$\rho_T=\frac{|\bar{V}_{F2}|}{|\bar{V}_{F1}|}$$
There is no constrain on voltages being conserved over transmission lines, but currents must be conserved. $|\bar{I}_{F2}|=|\bar{I}_{F1}|=|\bar{I}_{B1}|$
From this we can do some algebra and find that: $$\rho_T=\frac{Z_{02}}{Z_{01}}(1-\rho_L)=\frac{2Z_{02}}{Z_{02}+Z_{01}}$$
The impedance at any given point in the transmission line can be found by:
$$Z_{(x)}=\frac{\bar{V}_F e^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}  +\bar{V}_Be^{j(\omega t + \beta x)}}{\bar{I}_Fe^{j(\omega t - \beta x)}+ \bar{I}_Be^{j(\omega t + \beta x)}}=Z_0\frac{Z_L\cos(\beta x)-jZ_0\sin(\beta x)}{-jZ_L\sin(\beta x)+Z_0\cos(\beta x)}$$
The impedance looking into the line of length $l$ is $Z(x=-l)=Z_0\frac{Z_L+jZ_0\tan(\beta l)}{Z_0 + jZ_l \tan(\beta l)}$

**Quarter Wave Matching**
An interesting effect occurs when the line has length $l=\lambda / 4$
$Z(x=-\lambda / 4)=Z_0^2/Z_L$. We can use this for impedance matching at both the input and the output. $Z_0=\sqrt{Z_LZ_{in}}$ for this effect, where $Z_0$ is the characteristic impedance of the added quarter wave line

**Ringing**
When we try and transmit something like a square wave, we usually need time for the voltage to settle after each change

This is because we have $V_i \frac{Z_0}{Z_0+Z_i}$ volts going in, and at the far end we have reflection of $A=\frac{Z_L-Z_0}{Z_L+Z_0}$ and at the close end we have reflection of $A=\frac{Z_i-Z_0}{Z_i+Z_0}$
This repeats and gives us a series expression, $V(x=-l,t=2nT)=V_i (\frac{Z_0}{Z_0+Z_i})[1+\sum_{n=1}^n (\rho_L^n \rho_i^{n-1}+\rho_L^n \rho_i^{n})]$
This geometric series converges with time
