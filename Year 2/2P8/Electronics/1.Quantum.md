Everything has a particle-wave duality
$E=mc^2=hf$. Let c become the speed of the particle. $mv^2=hf$
Rearrange to give $p=h/\lambda$

General rule: If the physical size of the object is comparable to it's wavelength then you will notice quantum effects

k = wave number = $2\pi / \lambda$. Hence $p=mv=\frac{h}{2\pi}\times \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = \hbar k$
$\hbar$ is the reduced Plank constant, so we can write $E=hf=\hbar \omega$

We know the general solution of a wave equation will be a backwards and forwards travelling wave: $Ae^{j(\omega t - kx)}+Be^{j(\omega t + kx)}$

**Quantum Mechanics**
The wave function, $\psi$, contains information about all measurable parameters of the system like trajectory and energy and so on
$\psi^* \psi$ is the probability of finding the object between x and x+dx

We need to normalise $\psi$ by setting $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi|^2 dx = 1$

Correspondence - each classical variable has an equivalent quantum variable

Consider a plane wave solution:  $\psi(x,t) = A e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$
Take the spatial derivative:  $\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} = i k \psi$
$\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} = i \hbar k \psi$
Using $p = \hbar k$, substitute: $\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} = i p \psi$

Rearrange: $p \psi = -i \hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}$
By comparison, we identify the momentum operator:  $\hat{p} = -i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial x}$
Applying the operator twice:  $\hat{p}^2 = (-i\hbar)^2 \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} = -\hbar^2 \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}$

Setting kinetic energy + potential energy = total energy, we get the time dependent Schrodinger equation: $-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}+ V\psi = -\frac{\hbar}{i}\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t}$
In making time independent, we get this formula: $-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}+ V\psi = E\psi$

If V is constant, we get $\psi = A_1e^{i\alpha x}+B_1e^{-i\alpha x}$, where $\alpha^2 =\frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E-V)$
To find the coefficients we must apply the constraints: $\psi$ and $\frac{d\psi}{dx}$ must be continuous and of course the normalisation property

Whenever a quantum particle encounters any discontinuity in potential, it will be scattered.
If we get an exponential solution we can ignore the exponentially increasing part, as that is non-physical as we know that $\psi$ can't diverge to $\infty$.

If the total energy of the electron $E$ is less than the potential of the step $V$, we get $\psi_2(x)=A_2 e^{-k_2 x}$, so is an exponential decay.

We can get $A_1, B_1, B_2$ from boundary conditions, that is the value and first spatial derivative are continuous. The first spatial derivate must be continuous so there isn't a singularity in the second spatial derivative, which is essentially the energy.
From this we can get $B_1/A_1$ the reflection proportion, and $|B_1/A_1|^2$ the reflection probability, which is 1 in the case where $V>E$

If $E>V$, we get a standing wave to the left of the step, and a propagating wave to the right. This has a finite probability of reflection.

If we have a potential barrier, like a grain boundary, we have 3 distinct regions. Standing wave to the left, decay in the barrier (there is an exponentially increasing term but it is normally quite small), and then a propagating wave to the right.

This is quantum tunnelling. This is used in a scanning tunnelling microscope, where a metal tip is kept about a nanometre from the surface of a material, causing a small quantum tunnelling current. A PID controller maintains this current, and the tip scans over the atoms, so you can visualise them. Depending on whether the tip is positively or negatively biased with respect to the sample, we are either imaging filled or empty states of the sample.

Let's imagine instead we have an infinite potential well with an electron in it. As the potential is infinite we have zero probability of it being anywhere but the well.
The solution will be $\psi(x)=Ae^{ikx}+Be^{-ikx}$ where $k=\frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}$ where we are obviously ignoring the time dependence.

Using boundary conditions we get that $k=n\pi /L$, so $\psi(x)=A\sin(n\pi x/L)$, and A can be found by normalising.
It is clear we have different harmonics forming. 

All the electrons energy is kinetic, so $E=\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2m} = \frac{h^2n^2}{8mL^2}$
This clearly shows the energy levels are quantised. $n$ is the quantum number.

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle - If $\Delta x$ is the error in determining position, and $\Delta p$ is the error in determining momentum, $\Delta p\Delta x \geq \hbar / 2$

In an atom, electrons are bound by a Coulombic potential well. The angular velocity is quantised, and hence the radii are quantised.

**Atomic Structure**

Properties are determined by the valence electrons. Semiconductors have 4 electrons in outer shell, form 4 bonds and have a diamond structure.

Resistance arises from defects from perfect crystallinity, largely due to temperature.

We can also form compounds with an average of 4 electrons per atom, with the same diamond structure, now called zincblende.

Crystal planes are defined by Miller indices. We find where the plane cuts the axes, then take the reciprocals of these values. We then multiply through so they are integers, e.g. (2,1,4) plane. A 0 means it never cuts that axis.

We can determine atomic spacing using Bragg's law and electron/ X-ray diffraction.
Impinge a beam of high energy electrons on a crystal at an angle $\theta$ from the surface. 
There will be constructive interference when $n\lambda = 2d \sin(\theta)$.
The intensity spectrum against $\theta$ will have many maximas and minimas.

**Formation of Bands**

Pauli exclusion principle - no 2 electrons can have the exact same 4 quantum numbers. Therefore no energy level can have more than 2 electrons. One has up spin $1/2$ and the other has down spin $-1/2$. Spin explains why electrons are intrinsically magnetic.

When two atoms get too close, their energy levels would have to merge, but this cannot happen by the Pauli exclusion principle. Therefore they undergo energy level splitting. $n$ atoms will give you $n$ sub levels.
If we add more atoms, these split energy levels blend and look more like bands. Properties depend on band structure.

The valence band is the highest energy band filled with electrons, so can't move or flow. The conduction band is empty or partially filled, and is where electrons can conduct.
For a conductor, the bands overlap.
For an insulator, there is a large bandgap so electrons cannot reach the conduction band. The energy gap $E_g>2eV$
For a semiconductor, there is there is an energy gap $E_g$ between $0$ and $2eV$. The energy gap is also temperature dependent.

Electrons move with a random walk at the Fermi velocity
Parameters determining conductivity in a metal: Electron density, Drift velocity

In a semiconductor:
By the Boltzmann distribution, few electrons have sufficient energy to overcome the bandgap.
Conductivity depends on: 
* Free electron concentration, which depends on thermal energy and doping
* Hole concentration in the valence band
* Velocity of electrons when a field is applied
* Velocity of holes when a field is applied

If a semiconductor is intrinsic it means it is pure, and the number of holes per unit volume is the number of electrons per unit volume. $n=p=n_i$
Thermal equilibrium implies the only source of energy for electrons is thermal due to ambient temperature.

The number of free carriers per unit volume available for conduction at thermal equilibrium in intrinsic semiconductor is called the intrinsic carrier concentration $n_i$
This follows an Arrhenius law: $n_i \propto T^{3/2} e^{-\frac{E_g}{2k_B T}}$

We will consider the current as being dependent on: n, p, and the mobility of carriers $\mu$

If we dope, the semiconductor becomes extrinsic. It is always true that at equilibrium: $np = n_i^2$
If we do N-type doping, where the dopants are donors, we get $n=n_i + N_D$
If the doping concentration $N_D >> n_i$ then $p=n_i^2/N_D$
If we do P-type doping then the dopants are acceptors

When we apply a low electric field, the velocity (called drift velocity) $v_d$ is proportional to electric field. The constant of proportionality is the mobility $\mu$. $v_d=\mu E$

We can understand mobility better using a force balance were $m_n^*$ is the effective mass of the electron and $\tau$ is the time between collisions
$\frac{m_n^*  v_d}{\tau}=qE$ so $\mu = \frac{q\tau_n}{m_n^*}$, and similarly for holes. 

Holes move a lot slower as they require the deformation of a lattice, so more current is carried by electrons.

Instead, at high electric fields, the velocity saturated, and becomes dependent on the semiconductor.

Calculating drift current in a semiconductor:
Drift current density = $J_{dr}$, the charge per unit volume $\times$ the drift velocity
$J_{dr}=qnv_{dn}+qpv_{dp}=q(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)E$
$I=J_{dr}A=q(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)A\frac{dV}{dx}$

Separating variables and integrating gives: $V_a = \frac{1}{q(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)}\frac{L}{A}I$
So resistivity $\rho = \frac{1}{q(n\mu_n + p\mu_p)}$ and conductivity $\sigma = 1/\rho$

Once we have this value of resistivity and the dimensions of the problem we can work out the resistance.
This also allows us to calculate the effects of doping on the resistance. In this calculation we assume constant mobility, which isn't actually true as in reality it would decrease as other atoms are substituted into the lattice.

A direct bandgap semiconductor is one in which the top of the valence band is directly below the bottom of the conduction band on a energy-momentum graph. If not direct you have to change the momentum by changing the temperature.
