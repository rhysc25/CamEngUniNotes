One of the Maxwell equations: $\nabla \cdot E = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon}$. Combining with the fact that $E=-\frac{d\phi}{dx}$ where $\phi$ is the potential. This gives:  $\frac{d^2\phi}{dx^2}=-\frac{\rho}{\epsilon}$, which is Poisson's equation

Considering a parallel plate capacitor with large enough plates to avoid fringe effects, and remembering that $\rho=0$ in the middle, we get $E=\frac{V_a}{L}$ and $\phi=V_a-\frac{V_a}{L}x$

Now let's put semiconducting material in the capacitor. But first lets consider the types of charges in a semiconductor:
1) Mobile charges: electrons and holes
2) Fixed charges: e.g. Dopant ions. After doping, the dopants are ionised. In n-type doping, to give an electron, the dopant becomes a positive ion. Sometimes a region in a semiconductor might be depleted, meaning that it is nearly void of mobile carriers, so the only charges present are the dopant ions.

Consider a p-doped semiconductor with acceptor dopant concentration $N_A$. The region from $x=$ 0 to d is depleted.
$\rho = q(N_D+ p - N_A - n)$, which in this scenario is just given by $\rho = -qN_A$
Then just perform the necessary integration gives us: $E = \frac{qN_A}{\epsilon}(d-x)$
So $\phi = \frac{qN_A}{\epsilon}(\frac{x^2}{2}-xd)$

This tells us that to make a depletion region of $1\mu m$ we need $0.375V$

A MOSFET has a channel length L and a width W, so an aspect ratio W/L. It also has a slight overlap of the gate and the source and drain, an overlap length $L_{ov}$

Depletion mode MOSFET - the channel is lightly doped so even when gate voltage is 0V the transistor is on. When a gate voltage $<0V$ is applied, positive charge is pushed into the channel creating a depletion region.

As the gate voltage creates a vertical field but the $V_{ds}$ creates a horizontal field, this depletion region becomes skewed to one side. Mostly we will assume this effect is negligible.

For an enhancement mode there is no pre-doping, it is off when gate voltage = 0V. When a gate voltage $>0V$ is applied, an electron channel forms between the drain and source. 

We can apply similar maths to the depleted n-type channel region in a depletion mode MOSFET.
$E=\frac{qN_D}{\epsilon}x+C$.
Use the boundary condition that when $x=x_d$, $E=0$. No electric field as doped electric field is a conductor, so $E=\frac{qN_D}{\epsilon}(x-x_d)$

At high fields, velocity saturates. Thus, transit time $t$ and switching speed is limited. $I_{sat}=\frac{Q}{t}=\frac{qN_DWDL}{t}=qN_DWDv_s$

Thin Film Transistors (TFTs)
Current voltage characteristics similar to enhancement mode MOSFETs
At $V_{gs}=0$ there is no channel. If $V_{gs}>0$, there is a conductive channel of electrons between the source and drain, and the potential within the channel $V_c(y)$ varies from source to drain.

$\frac{I_{ds}}{Wh}=qnv_d=qn\mu \frac{\partial V_c}{\partial y}$
We need an expression for $qn$ so we consider $C_{ox}$, the insulator capacitance per unit area.
$qn = \frac{C_{ox}((V_{gs}-V_T)-V_c(y))}{h}$ 
Here $(V_{gs}-V_T)$ is clearly the voltage above the threshold value

Combining and solving the differential equation we get $I_{ds}=\mu \frac{W}{L}C_{ox}((V_{gs}-V_T)V_{ds}-\frac{V_{ds}^2}{2})$
This is true only if the transistor is in linear operation where $V_{ds}<V_{gs}-V_T$, where the transistor behaves likes a resistor.

If $V_{ds}>V_{gs}-V_T$ we get pinch off, where the current saturates and becomes independent of $V_{ds}$. This is saturation operation. It behaves like a gate controlled current source. Replace $V_{ds}=V_{gs}-V_T$ in the equation and we get a strain line when we plot $I_{ds}$ vs $V_{ds}$

Metal semiconductor FET (MESFET)
This is like a MOSFET but with no insulator between gate and semiconductor
The metal diffuses electrons into the semiconductor, creating a Schottky contact, essentially a PN junction.

**Scaling of Transistors**
Scaling implies reducing L, W and $t_{ox}$ by a factor $\alpha$
However scaling down $t_{ox}$ will increase quantum tunnelling, so increase permittivity of the dielectric.
Two methods of scaling: 
1) Constant field scaling - scale down applied voltages by $1/\alpha$ per transistor. The power transistor scales by $1/\alpha^2$, but you can fit $\alpha^2$ more transistors in a given area, so total power conserved. Voltage levels not compatible previous versions.
2) Constant voltage scaling. Compatible with previous versions. However power per transistor increases by factor $\alpha$
