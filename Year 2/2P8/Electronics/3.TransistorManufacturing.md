Silicon should not be the best, as it has a low carrier mobility so low switching speed, and an indirect gap unsuitable for most optoelectronic applications.
However, it is still used as it is super cheap, forms good quality insulators, and because we have so much experience with it.

Silicon is group 4, diamond structure, high mp, strong, but quite brittle

We need very pure single crystal silicon, so we use the Czochralski process
First we react SiC with SiO2 to make Si, but this is very pure so we make it into a liquid by reacting with 3HCl and then fractionally distil it, giving pure SiHCl3. 
Then react this with H2 to give Si. This still has a lot of defects though, so we melt into a silica crucible, which rotates.

We then dip a seed crystal of known orientation into the crucible and extract slowly so produce a solid single crystal ingot.

To produce a higher purity material, the float zone growth technique can be used, we we melt the polycrystalline silicon locally with an rf heating element. 
The orientation of the crystal is determined by x-ray diffraction and flat edges ground onto the ingot which can be later used to determine the doping and orientation of the ingot.

The edges are rounded to reduce the risk of cracking.

Epitaxial growth allows layers of Si to be grown on top of a wafer.
In vapour phase epitaxy (VPE), the silicon wafer is heated to high temp in an atmosphere of some Silicon gas
Molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) is where a stream of heated Si atoms are shot at the surface

For a MOSFET we need a p doped region on the n doped region. We could add dopant into the feed for epitaxial growth. This doesn't really embed it though.
A better way is by using diffusion of dopants into the bulk silicon. 
Assuming a constant concentration at the surface this gives us the error function as the solution to our equation.

An alternative to dopant diffusion is ion implantation, which is used to really embed the dopant, forming a Gaussian distribution within the material, at a depth $R_p$
This is governed by this equation $N_i(x)=\frac{Q_i}{\sqrt{2\pi}\Delta R_p}exp[\frac{-1}{2}(\frac{x-R_p}{\Delta R_p})^2]$

**Silicon Dioxide**
This is an excellent insulator and is readily etched by HF. It is an amorphous material, and forms open rings, easy for small elements to diffuse in.

To form SiO2 we use thermal oxidation. We can do it dry: Si + O2 -> SiO2
Or wet: Si + 2H2O -> SiO2 + H2
This is conversion of Si to SiO2, not growth.

If we wanted growth, we could use rf-PECVD. However with this method, the steps edges tend to be non-conformal, so the thicknesses aren't uniform.
This is solved by LPCVD.
In recent years Atomic Layer Deposition has also arose, a cyclic deposition process where reactants are introduced and purged in order. 

We also need to think about placing metal tracks which are our conductive pathways
One way is through evaporation - heat a metal in a crucible of a metal with a higher melting point until they evaporate onto another surface.

Flux of atoms evaporating per second $F=N_0exp(\frac{-\phi_e}{kT})$
$N_0$ is a slowly varying function of temperature. $\phi_e$ is the activation energy required to evaporate one molecule, related to the enthalpy of formation of the evaporant by $\phi_e=H/N_A$ 
The source to substrate distance should be small compared with the mean free path in the vacuum. However can't get too close as that would heat the material.
We also need the time required for a monolayer of the residual vacuum material to hit the substrate surface should be long compared with the time for a monolayer of material to be evaporated.

Evaporation results in line of sight deposition.
Deposition rate, R: $R\propto \frac{\cos(\beta)\cos(\theta)}{d^2}$

Therefore deposition is thickest in the middle of the wafer.

We could also heat the metal with an electron beam, laser heating, or rf induction heating.

The most popular method for producing metallic layers - sputtering 
Ions of an inert gas are fired at a block of the desired source material under vacuum, passing their kinetic energy to atoms at the target surface.
Can also add gas into the system to change the stoichiometry of the deposited layer.

The sputter yield is the number of atoms removed per incident ion. Most metals have a sputter yield of ~1.
Dependent on: Ion size (normally argon), Target material, Ion energy, Angle of incidence
![[Pasted image 20260520175424.png]]
There is an optimal sputtering angle, to not embed the ions, and so they don't glance off.

Deposition rate can be quite slow.
Make sure the target is big, almost similar to the substrate, such that the covering is more even. Better conformality.

Electroplating - dip in a solution of metal ions you want to deposit. Make the wafer the cathode with a DC bias.
Make the anode out of the material you want to deposit, so the concentration of the solution is roughly constant.

From the Faraday law of electrolysis, the mass of metal deposited per unit area per unit time, M, is given by $M=\frac{JA}{ZF}$ where A and Z are the atomic weight and valency respectively, and F is the Faraday constant.

If we want a transparent conducting oxide, we most of the time use indium tin oxide.

How to characterise the material you produce -
Microscopy
Physical characterisation, e.g. x-ray diffraction
Electron characterisation

Optical microscopy - The resolving power is given by $\delta = \frac{C\lambda}{n\sin(\alpha)}$ where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the illumination, n is the refractive index of the medium between the sample and the objective, $\alpha$ is the semi-angle subtended by the objective at the sample and C is the coherence constant, normally 0.61.

The limitation here is the wavelength of the light. Therefore we use electron microscopy. Almost identical to the workings of the optical microscope except using electrons.

Profilometry - a sharp tip is pressed onto the sample surface with a known force and scanned over the surface.
Can also do optical profilometry with a white light source and inferometer.

X-ray diffraction
The Bragg equation - constructive interference only occurs when $n\lambda = 2d\sin\theta$
Diffraction can occur from any set of planes. A plane is defined by its Miller indices ($h,k,l$): the number of times the plane cuts each side of the unit cell.
For a given set of planes in a cubic unit cell with side lengths a,b and c, the plane separation will be $\frac{1}{d^2}=\frac{h^2}{a^2}+\frac{k^2}{b^2}+\frac{l^2}{c^2}$

Different crystal structures have different combinations of peaks. Allows you to find out structure, and interatomic spacing.

**Patterning**
Instead of just carpet covering the surface, we now need to think about defining certain areas where material is and isn't, known as pattern transfer. 
This is done through photolithography

**Photolithography**
A material that is sensitive to high energy photons or electrons (the photoresist) on the surface of a sample is exposed to a pattern of photons/electrons
Presupposes that the material is clean and free from particulates, so takes place in a clean room environment, where the air entering has been passed through High Efficiency Particle Arrestors
We then classify how clean it is by the concentration/number of particles of diameter greater than a certain D

**Substrate Cleanliness**
RCA cleaning: Both RCA1 and RCA2 involve immersing the wafer in a boiling solution
HF cleaning: dip in buffered hydrofluoric acid
Fuming nitric acid: immerse in fuming nitric acid
Solvent clean

**Photoresist**
The photoresist is an organic polymer resin in a liquid solvent.
A thin coating of photoresist may be formed by a process known as spin coating - the sample is rotated and a polymer solution is applied to the centre of the sample
The empirical expression for the thickness $t=\frac{KC^{\beta}\eta^{\gamma}}{R^{\alpha}}$
C is polymer concentration, $\eta$ is intrinsic viscosity, R is rotations per minute, K is a calibration constant, and $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are constants.

Broadly classified into two categories: 
Positive tone resists - High energy photons cause scission of the polymer chains
Negative tone resists - High energy photons cause cross-linking of the chains to create large molecular weight chains which are insoluble

Positive tone resists get removed upon photons incident, whereas negative tone resists build up

The scattering of photons at the edge of features can never be fully avoided, and this results in developed features having characteristic profiles

The actual profile of the side wall will be dependent on both the exposure and the development.

For positive resists, the resist contrast is dependent upon the rate of chain scission and the rate of change of polymer solubility with molecular weight.
Photon doses below a certain level $D_P^0$ will have no effect upon the resist. As the photon dose D applied to the resist increases, the resist thickness after development will decrease until the critical dose $D_P$ is reached, at which point the resist is removed.
The contrast is determined from the gradient of normalised resist thickness against $\log(D)$
$\gamma=\frac{1}{\log(D_P)-\log(D_P^0)}=[\log(\frac{D_P}{D_P^0})]^{-1}$

**UV Photolithography**
Three basic methods for producing a UV image on the photoresist:
1) Proximity printing - a mask produces a shadow on the sample which is above the sample
2) Contact printing - a mask produces a shadow on the sample which is in contact with the sample
3) Projection printing - the pattern on a large mask is projected onto the sample, and stepped over the full area
The critical dimension of a particular lithographic process is the minimum feature size which can be reliably produced

The resolution for shadow printing using a conventional resist of thickness z and with a print gap between the mask and the resist surface is given by:
$R=\frac{3}{2}\sqrt{\lambda(s+\frac{z}{2})}$
So why would we use proximity printing over contact printing? Contact printing will degrade the mask.

Projection printing resolution is limited by the diffraction of light.
The resolution of a projection printing system is approximated by $R=\frac{k_1\lambda}{N}$ where N is the numerical aperture of the system $N=n\sin(\theta_{max})$

X-ray lithography is an extension of UV lithography.
E-beam lithography required no mask as already precise

All layers in the process must be aligned, so we have crosses which are regularly repeated over the mask and the sample and must be overlayed for alignment.

In practice it is never possible to perfectly align two layers to each other so we need a tolerance. Self-alignment provides a cunning way of reducing this problem by using the pattern on the substrate to act as the mask itself for the next stage of patterning.

**Etching**
Using photolithography we have formed a patterned polymer layer on the surface of the sample. We still require some means by which this pattern can be transferred to the sample itself.
Etching comes in two categories:
Wet etching: Immersion of the substrate in a liquid
Dy etching: Exposure to reactive species in the gas phase

**Wet etching**
Three main steps:
1) Transportation of reactants to the surface to be etched
2) Chemical reaction between the reactants and the surface
3) Transportation of reactant products away from the surface
If step 1 rate limiting then said to be mass transfer limited
If step 2 rate limiting then said to be reaction rate limited

Etching characterised by:
1) Etching rate
2) Etch selectivity
3) Etch uniformity

Isotropic etching - they etch all crystal planes of a material at the same rate
Assuming the masking material is completely resistant to the etch, isotropic wet etching produces a very characteristic undercut profile.

For example if we wanted a silicon cantilever we would need a sacrificial material that can be removed from underneath the cantilever without attacking the Si, so SiO2 that is readily attacked by buffered HF.

Full process:
We have a silicon wafer with a later of silicon oxide on it, a layer of silicon, then our photoresist.
Pattern the photoresist then etch the silicon layer. Then etch the SiO2 layer with bHF to undercut the cantilever. Then remove the photoresist.

Anisotropic etching is where the wet chemical etch rate varies with crystallographic orientation.
Orientation dependence is a consequence of the fact that different crystalline surfaces will have varying structures.
This can allow for the fabrication of free standing structures without the need for a sacrificial layer between the superstructure and the substrate.

**Etch stops**
If a wet etching process has been well characterised, then it should be possible to etch features of a certain depth knowing the etching rate.
In practice it is more difficult as the effective concentration of the etchant my decrease with use.
If the depth of a feature is a critical parameter, some method of precisely controlling the end of an etch - an etch stop process - will be necessary.

**Dielectric etch stop**
A dielectric layer can be introduced underneath the layer to be etched to ensure that the etch terminates at the correct depth, as dielectrics tend to have a reduced etch rate compared to silicon.
**Doping selective etching (DSE)**
Silicon heavily doped with boron also has a significantly reduced anisotropic etch rate. However this increases intrinsic stress which limits the use of these layers as mechanical components.
**Bias selective etching (BSE)**
A positively biased silicon wafer will be oxidised in an etchant solution to form a protective oxide layer which will prevent etching.

**Dry etching**
The use of gas phase etchants. The use of a continuous flow of gas naturally removes unwanted reaction products from the surface. Also ensures constant etch rate.

**Vapour etching**
Xenon difluoride
**Reactive ion etching (RIE)**
The reactant gas is excited to form a plasma which contains the reactive species required to perform the etch. The bombardment of the surface top being etched by ions from the plasma can lead to sputter enhancement of the etch rate.
**Deep reactive ion etching (DRIE)**
Very slow, not valid. Produces deep structures with intact vertical sidewalls.
